Intermittent Fasting (IF) — Protocols, Mechanisms, and Evidence

Intermittent fasting refers to eating patterns that cycle between periods of fasting and eating. Unlike traditional diets, IF focuses on when to eat rather than what to eat.

Common IF protocols:

16:8 (Leangains protocol):
Fast for 16 hours, eat within an 8-hour window. Most popular approach. Example: eat between 12:00-20:00, fast from 20:00-12:00. Black coffee and plain tea are permitted during the fasting window.

5:2 diet:
Eat normally 5 days per week, restrict to 500-600 kcal on 2 non-consecutive days. Developed by Dr. Michael Mosley. The 500-600 kcal should come from lean protein and vegetables on fasting days.

OMAD (One Meal A Day):
All daily calories consumed in a single meal within 1-2 hours. Very aggressive approach, typically 23:1 fast-to-eating ratio. Can be difficult to meet protein and micronutrient targets.

Alternate Day Fasting (ADF):
Alternate between normal eating days and fasting days (either complete fast or 25% of normal calories). Difficult to sustain long-term but shows strong metabolic results.

Eat Stop Eat:
One or two 24-hour fasts per week. Proposed by Brad Pilon. Example: fast from dinner Monday to dinner Tuesday.

Physiological mechanisms:

Insulin reduction:
During fasting, insulin levels drop dramatically, which facilitates fat mobilisation. Fasting insulin levels typically fall 20-31% within 24 hours of fasting.

Growth hormone increase:
Fasting increases growth hormone secretion up to 5-fold in some studies, which helps preserve lean muscle mass during caloric restriction.

Autophagy:
Cellular self-cleaning process activated during fasting, particularly after 16-24 hours. Autophagy removes damaged organelles and proteins. This is a key proposed mechanism for IF's longevity and disease prevention effects. Activates via AMPK and mTOR inhibition.

Metabolic switching:
After 12-16 hours of fasting, the body switches from glucose to fat and ketones as primary fuel. This metabolic flexibility is associated with improved insulin sensitivity.

Evidence for IF:

Weight loss:
IF produces comparable weight loss to continuous caloric restriction when calories are matched. A 2020 meta-analysis of 27 trials found IF reduced body weight by 0.8-13% over 8-24 weeks. Most of this is through reduced overall caloric intake rather than a unique metabolic mechanism.

Metabolic health:
IF reduces fasting insulin (20-31%), blood glucose (3-6%), and blood pressure in overweight individuals. Particularly effective for people with insulin resistance or prediabetes.

Cardiovascular markers:
Reduces LDL cholesterol, triglycerides, and inflammatory markers (IL-6, CRP) in multiple studies.

Cognitive function:
Animal studies show strong neuroprotective effects. Human evidence is limited but suggests improved focus and mental clarity, possibly from ketone production during the fasted state.

Muscle preservation:
IF with adequate protein intake (1.6-2.2g/kg/day) does not cause greater muscle loss than continuous restriction. Resistance training during IF helps preserve lean mass.

Practical tips for 16:8:
- Break the fast with a protein-rich meal (eggs, chicken, Greek yogurt)
- Training: either fasted (for fat oxidation) or 1-2 hours after breaking the fast
- Protein target: hit your full daily protein target within the eating window
- Electrolytes: take sodium, potassium, magnesium if feeling fatigued during extended fasts
- Black coffee and tea: permitted and may enhance fat oxidation and autophagy during the fasting window

Who should avoid IF:
- Pregnant or breastfeeding women
- People with a history of eating disorders
- Type 1 diabetics (without medical supervision)
- Children and teenagers
- Those who are underweight

Combining IF with training:
For strength training, protein synthesis is maximised when protein is consumed within the eating window. Pre- and post-workout nutrition remains important — aim for 30-40g leucine-rich protein (whey, chicken, eggs) around training sessions.
