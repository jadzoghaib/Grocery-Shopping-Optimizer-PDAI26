Nutrition Basics — Macronutrients, Micronutrients, and Energy

ENERGY BALANCE

Total Daily Energy Expenditure (TDEE):
TDEE = BMR × Activity Multiplier

Basal Metabolic Rate (BMR) — Mifflin-St Jeor equation (most accurate):
Men:   BMR = (10 × weight_kg) + (6.25 × height_cm) − (5 × age) + 5
Women: BMR = (10 × weight_kg) + (6.25 × height_cm) − (5 × age) − 161

Activity multipliers:
- Sedentary (desk job, no exercise): BMR × 1.2
- Lightly active (1-3 days/week exercise): BMR × 1.375
- Moderately active (3-5 days/week): BMR × 1.55
- Very active (6-7 days/week hard training): BMR × 1.725
- Extremely active (physical job + daily training): BMR × 1.9

Example — 75 kg, 180 cm, 30-year-old male, moderately active:
BMR = (750) + (1125) − (150) + 5 = 1,730 kcal
TDEE = 1,730 × 1.55 = 2,682 kcal/day

Energy from macronutrients:
- Carbohydrates: 4 kcal/g
- Protein: 4 kcal/g
- Fat: 9 kcal/g
- Alcohol: 7 kcal/g (no nutritional function; not a macronutrient)

MACRONUTRIENTS

PROTEIN

Functions: muscle synthesis and repair, enzyme production, immune function, hormone synthesis, satiety.

Daily requirements:
- Sedentary adult: 0.8 g/kg/day (RDA minimum)
- Active adult: 1.2-1.6 g/kg/day
- Muscle building: 1.6-2.2 g/kg/day
- Cutting/deficit: 2.2-3.1 g/kg/day (higher to prevent muscle loss)
- Elderly (65+): 1.2-1.6 g/kg/day (counteract sarcopenia)

Protein quality:
- Complete proteins (all 9 essential amino acids): meat, fish, eggs, dairy, soy, quinoa
- Incomplete proteins (missing ≥1 EAA): most plant proteins (rice, beans, peas)
- Complementary proteins: rice + beans, hummus + pita — combine to form complete profile

Leucine threshold: ~2.5-3 g leucine per meal triggers maximum muscle protein synthesis (MPS). This corresponds to ~20-35g protein from high-quality sources per meal.

High-protein foods per 100g:
Chicken breast: 31g | Tuna canned: 26g | Cod: 18g | Greek yogurt: 10g | Lentils: 9g | Eggs: 13g | Cottage cheese: 11g | Tofu: 8g

CARBOHYDRATES

Functions: primary energy source for brain and muscles, glycogen storage, fibre for gut health.

Types:
- Simple carbohydrates (fast-digesting): glucose, fructose, sucrose — found in fruit, sugar, white bread, sweets. Rapid insulin spike.
- Complex carbohydrates (slow-digesting): starch, glycogen — found in oats, rice, potato, legumes. Sustained energy.
- Dietary fibre: insoluble (gut motility, found in wheat bran, vegetables) + soluble (lowers LDL cholesterol, found in oats, beans, apples)

Daily requirements:
- Sedentary adult: 3-4 g/kg/day
- Endurance athlete: 5-7 g/kg/day
- High-intensity training: 6-10 g/kg/day
- Minimum (brain function): ~130 g/day

Glycaemic Index (GI):
- High GI (>70): white bread (75), rice cakes (82), glucose (100)
- Medium GI (55-70): brown rice (68), banana (62), oats (57)
- Low GI (<55): lentils (32), chickpeas (28), apple (36), sweet potato (44)

Fibre targets: 25-35 g/day. Most adults consume <15 g/day.

FATS

Functions: hormone production (testosterone, estrogen), fat-soluble vitamin absorption (A, D, E, K), cell membrane structure, brain function (~60% of brain is fat), long-term energy storage.

Types:
- Saturated fats: butter, coconut oil, red meat, dairy. Raise LDL cholesterol when consumed in excess. Limit to <10% of total calories.
- Monounsaturated fats (MUFA): olive oil, avocado, almonds. Reduce LDL, maintain HDL. Heart-protective.
- Polyunsaturated fats (PUFA): omega-3 (EPA, DHA, ALA) and omega-6 (linoleic acid).
  - Omega-3 (anti-inflammatory): fatty fish (salmon, sardines, mackerel), walnuts, flaxseeds, chia seeds. Target: 2-3 g EPA+DHA/day for active individuals.
  - Omega-6 (pro-inflammatory in excess): sunflower oil, corn oil, soybean oil. Western diet omega-6:omega-3 ratio is ~15:1; target is ~4:1.
- Trans fats: partially hydrogenated oils in processed foods. Raise LDL + lower HDL. No safe threshold — avoid entirely.

Daily fat targets:
- General adult: 0.8-1.2 g/kg/day (20-35% of total calories)
- Keto diet: 70-80% of calories from fat
- Low-fat diet: 20-25% of calories from fat

Essential fatty acids (must be obtained from diet):
- Linoleic acid (omega-6): found in most plant oils
- Alpha-linolenic acid (omega-3): found in flaxseeds, chia, walnuts

MICRONUTRIENTS — KEY VITAMINS

Vitamin D:
- Functions: calcium absorption, bone density, immune function, testosterone production
- Deficiency: common in northern latitudes; linked to depression, fatigue, poor immunity
- Sources: sunlight (primary), fatty fish, egg yolks, fortified milk
- Target: 1,000-4,000 IU/day supplement in winter

Vitamin B12:
- Functions: DNA synthesis, red blood cell formation, nerve function
- Deficiency risk: vegans, vegetarians, elderly (reduced absorption), metformin users
- Sources: meat, fish, eggs, dairy, nutritional yeast (fortified)
- Target: 2.4 mcg/day (often 500-1,000 mcg supplement for vegans)

Iron:
- Functions: oxygen transport (haemoglobin), energy production, immune function
- Deficiency: most common nutritional deficiency globally; causes anaemia, fatigue
- Heme iron (well-absorbed): red meat, liver, shellfish
- Non-heme iron (less absorbed): spinach, lentils, fortified cereals
- Tip: vitamin C (e.g., lemon juice) increases non-heme iron absorption by 2-3×
- Target: 8 mg/day (men), 18 mg/day (women aged 19-50)

Vitamin C:
- Functions: collagen synthesis, immune function, antioxidant, iron absorption
- Sources: bell peppers, kiwi, strawberries, citrus, broccoli
- Target: 75-90 mg/day; smokers +35 mg/day

Folate (B9):
- Functions: DNA synthesis, cell division, neural tube development
- Critical for pregnancy: 400-800 mcg/day preconception and first trimester
- Sources: leafy greens, lentils, beans, fortified cereals

MICRONUTRIENTS — KEY MINERALS

Calcium:
- Functions: bone and teeth structure, muscle contraction, nerve signalling
- Sources: dairy, fortified plant milks, sardines (with bones), kale, broccoli, almonds
- Target: 1,000 mg/day (adults), 1,200 mg/day (women 50+, men 70+)
- Note: vitamin D required for absorption; caffeine and excess sodium reduce absorption

Magnesium:
- Functions: 300+ enzyme reactions, muscle relaxation, sleep quality, insulin sensitivity
- Deficiency: common; linked to muscle cramps, poor sleep, anxiety
- Sources: pumpkin seeds, almonds, dark chocolate (70%+), spinach, black beans
- Target: 310-420 mg/day; 300-400 mg glycinate supplement often useful for athletes

Potassium:
- Functions: blood pressure regulation (counters sodium), muscle function, heart rhythm
- Sources: bananas, avocado, sweet potato, spinach, white beans, salmon
- Target: 3,500-4,700 mg/day (most adults get ~2,500 mg)

Zinc:
- Functions: immune function, wound healing, testosterone production, protein synthesis
- Sources: oysters (highest), red meat, pumpkin seeds, legumes
- Target: 8-11 mg/day; athletes may need more due to sweat losses

HYDRATION

Water requirements:
- General guideline: 35 ml/kg body weight per day
- 75 kg adult: ~2.6 L/day from all sources (food contributes ~20%)
- Exercise: add 500-750 ml per hour of moderate exercise
- Dehydration of just 2% body weight impairs cognitive and physical performance

Electrolytes during exercise:
- Sodium: 500-1,000 mg/hour during prolonged exercise (>60 min)
- Potassium: banana or sports drink post-workout
- Magnesium: important for muscle relaxation; depleted by heavy sweating

MEAL TIMING

Pre-workout nutrition (1-3 hours before):
- Complex carbs: fuel glycogen stores (oats, rice, banana)
- Moderate protein: 20-30g to prime MPS
- Low fat: slows gastric emptying, can cause discomfort
- Example: chicken with rice, or oats with protein powder

Post-workout nutrition (within 2 hours):
- Protein: 20-40g fast-absorbing protein (whey, chicken, eggs) to initiate MPS
- Carbohydrates: 0.5-1 g/kg to replenish glycogen
- The "anabolic window" is wider than once thought — overall daily protein intake matters more than exact timing
- Example: whey shake + banana, or Greek yogurt with berries and granola

Protein distribution:
- Distributing protein across 3-5 meals optimises MPS vs. one large meal
- Each meal should contain ≥2.5g leucine to trigger MPS maximally
- 30-40g protein per meal is the practical target for most adults

FIBRE AND GUT HEALTH

Dietary fibre targets: 25-35 g/day

Soluble fibre (fermented by gut bacteria → short-chain fatty acids):
- Sources: oats, barley, apples, psyllium husk, legumes, flaxseeds
- Benefits: lowers LDL cholesterol, slows glucose absorption, feeds beneficial bacteria (prebiotic effect)

Insoluble fibre (bulking, accelerates transit):
- Sources: wheat bran, whole grains, vegetables, nuts
- Benefits: prevents constipation, reduces colorectal cancer risk

Prebiotic foods (feed beneficial bacteria): garlic, onions, leeks, asparagus, bananas, oats
Probiotic foods (introduce beneficial bacteria): yogurt, kefir, sauerkraut, kimchi, miso

READING FOOD LABELS

Per 100g is the most useful comparison unit — ignore "per serving" which varies by brand.

Traffic light labelling (per 100g):
- Fat: Low <3g | High >17.5g
- Saturated fat: Low <1.5g | High >5g
- Sugar: Low <5g | High >22.5g
- Salt: Low <0.3g | High >1.5g

Ingredient list: ingredients listed in descending order by weight. If sugar appears in the top 3 ingredients — the product is high in sugar. Watch for sugar aliases: glucose syrup, maltose, dextrose, fructose, corn syrup, agave nectar, fruit juice concentrate.

"Low fat" ≠ healthy: low-fat products often compensate with added sugar. Always check the full label.

COMMON NUTRITION MYTHS

Myth: Eating fat makes you fat.
Fact: Excess calories (from any macronutrient) cause fat gain. Healthy fats (avocado, olive oil, nuts) are essential for hormonal and cardiovascular health.

Myth: Carbs at night cause weight gain.
Fact: Total daily caloric balance determines body composition — not meal timing. Eating carbs at night is fine if within TDEE.

Myth: You need 8 glasses of water per day.
Fact: Water needs vary by body size, activity, climate, and diet. Use thirst and urine colour (pale yellow = adequate) as guides.

Myth: Protein is bad for your kidneys.
Fact: High protein (up to 2.5 g/kg) is safe for healthy adults with normal kidney function. Caution is warranted only in existing kidney disease.

Myth: Detox diets cleanse the body.
Fact: The liver, kidneys, and lymphatic system continuously detoxify the body. No juice cleanse or supplement improves this process.
